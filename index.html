Indicator: Age Structure
Age is grouped into five-year intervals, ranging from individuals aged 0-4 to 95-99. For each age group, we document the proportion of the population that is male versus the proportion of the population that is female.

Indicator: Gender of Household Head
We document the gender of the head of each surveyed household.

Indicator: Religion
We document the religion practiced by the head of each surveyed household. The majority of households in the Bird’s Head Seascape are either Christian or Muslim, with a small percentage of households practicing other religions (namely Buddhism or Hinduism).

Indicator: Ethnicity
The Bird’s Head Seascape is ethnically and linguistically diverse (Ananta et al. 2016). We document both paternal and maternal ethnicity for the head of each surveyed household, and report paternal ethnicity in Figure 5.
HUMAN WELL-BEING.
We synthesize data on five attributes of human well-being commonly identified in human development policy goals, identifying one or more leading indicators for each of these dimensions:

ECONOMIC WELL-BEING: the resources people use to meet basic consumption and material needs, and access other sources of well-being (Sen 1999)
Indicator: Primary Occupation
We document the primary occupation (i.e., the main way a household meets their needs) in the MPA, classifying households into seven occupational categories including farming (i.e., growing crops or raising livestock); harvesting forest products (e.g., timber, charcoal, non-timber forest products), fishing (e.g., capture of fish, crustaceans and other marine resources for sale or consumption), aquaculture (e.g., farmed fish, shrimp or seaweed); the extraction of non-renewable marine resources (e.g., sand mining; coral harvesting and mining); marine tourism (e.g., scuba dive operations), and other wage labor (e.g., medical or education professionals, government officials).

Indicator: Household Material Assets
Material assets are a reliable and widely used indicator of economic well-being. We adopt a standard ‘basket of goods’ methodology that assesses whether or not a household owns a particular asset (e.g., car, boat, telephone, television). The eleven-item ‘basket’ includes a range of goods, from low cost items (e.g., mobile phones) to high cost items (e.g., cars, boats with inboard motors). Each asset type is weighted according to its cost, with high value items given greater weight. We calculate a weighted sum of all the assets each household owns.

Indicator: Market Access
Market access (i.e. place that most households in the settlement go to sell all their goods) shapes both human well-being, marine resource use, and the condition of marine ecosystems (Cinner 2009). We document household market access by asking how long it usually takes to travel to each household’s respective primary market.

HEALTH: the state of complete physical, mental and social well-being, and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity (World Health Organization, 1946)
Indicator: Household Food Security Food security is the ability for all people, at all times, to access nutritionally adequate and safe food for an active, healthy life, in a socially acceptable way (Bickel et al. 2000). The Bird’s Head Seascape social monitoring program adopts an internationally recognized standard scale that classifies households into one of three food security categories (Bickel et al. 2000):

Food secure: Households show no or minimal evidence of food insecurity, meaning that they can access sufficient nutritionally adequate and safe food, in a socially acceptable way.

Food insecure without hunger: Food insecurity is evident in household members’ concerns about adequacy of the household food supply and in adjustments to household food management, including reduced quality of food and increased unusual coping patterns. Little or no reduction in members’ food intake is reported.
